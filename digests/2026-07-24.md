## [Notes to Self: Can LLMs Benefit from Experiential Abstractions?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.20372v1)
**Score:** 0.923  |  **arXiv:** 2607.20372v1

This paper investigates whether Large Language Models (LLMs) can improve their problem-solving by distilling past reasoning attempts into a retrievable library of reusable, natural-language strategies. By testing these abstractions during inference-time retrieval and reinforcement learning, the authors demonstrate consistent performance gains across mathematical and logical reasoning benchmarks. Notably, self-extracted abstractions perform as well as teacher-derived ones and transfer across different models and datasets, showing that LLMs can independently generate and apply their own experiential knowledge.

**Score:** 0.910  |  **arXiv:** 2607.19338v1

This paper introduces CodeRescue, a recovery framework for coding agents that dynamically decides whether a failed attempt should retry using a cheap model with execution feedback or escalate to a stronger, more expensive model. By combining a supervised router with a Conformal Risk Control layer, the system adapts its recovery decisions to varying cost budgets at runtime without requiring retraining. Across multiple coding benchmarks, CodeRescue matches or exceeds the solve rate of an "always-escalate" strategy while reducing recovery costs by up to 65%.

## [Selective State-Space Adaptation and Retrieval for Language Model Reasoning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19326v1)
**Score:** 0.906  |  **arXiv:** 2607.19326v1

To overcome standard LoRA's static updates, this paper introduces state-space adapters that dynamically adjust parameter updates at both the token and context levels using Mamba recurrence. Specifically, **MaLoRA** provides input-dependent dynamic scaling across tokens, while **MaRA** tracks cross-segment state to retrieve relevant context before generation. Evaluated across Qwen-2.5, Llama-3.1, and Gemma-2 backbones, the approach consistently outperforms standard LoRA on multi-hop reasoning and long-context QA benchmarks, yielding an average improvement of +6.8 F1.

## [Copy Less, Ground More: Overcoming Repetitive Copying in Long-Context Reasoning via Evidence-Aware Reinforcement Learning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19345v1)
**Score:** 0.905  |  **arXiv:** 2607.19345v1

The authors identify "repetitive copying"—where LLMs slavishly copy text from the input into their reasoning traces instead of solving the task—as a major failure mode in long-context reasoning driven by poor evidence grounding. To fix this, they introduce GEAR, a reinforcement learning method that uses automated data annotation to reward models for grounding their reasoning in key task-relevant evidence while penalizing overlap with irrelevant distractors. Across multiple benchmarks and context lengths, GEAR consistently reduces unhelpful copying and reasoning length while improving accuracy by up to 4.6 points over standard RL.

## [ISO: An RLVR-Native Optimization Stack](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.19331v1)
**Score:** 0.879  |  **arXiv:** 2607.19331v1

This paper introduces Isospectral Optimization (ISO), a framework tailored for post-training reinforcement learning that updates only a model's singular frames while keeping its base singular spectrum fixed. ISO includes an online optimizer that dramatically speeds up training—achieving target reasoning accuracy in under half the steps of standard AdamW—and a data-free offline merger for combining specialist models without needing post-merge rollouts or updates. It offers a principled, highly efficient alternative to applying standard pre-training optimizers wholesale to reward-driven post-training.

## [Train the Model, Not the Reader: Decodability Supervision for Verifiable Activation Explanations](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.20379v1)
**Score:** 0.875  |  **arXiv:** 2607.20379v1

**Summary for Researchers:**

Current methods for explaining AI activations rely on reconstruction metrics that only capture general "gist," making them blind to individual false claims and deceptive "private codes." To fix this, the authors introduce RECAP, a training technique that uses auxiliary linear heads to ensure specific internal features remain independently checkable without sacrificing model performance. In experiments, RECAP eliminates unfaithful encoding tricks and allows simple probes to reliably flag adversarial lies in generated explanations (AUC 0.95 vs. 0.51 for standard models).
