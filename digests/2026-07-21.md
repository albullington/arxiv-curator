## [When Do Multi-Agent Systems Help? An Information Bottleneck Perspective](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16133v1)
**Score:** 0.909  |  **arXiv:** 2607.16133v1

This paper introduces an information bottleneck framework to explain when and why multi-agent systems (MAS) outperform single-agent systems (SAS) in LLM tasks. The authors demonstrate that MAS limits context size through compressed relay messages, creating a fundamental trade-off where reducing context redundancy improves efficiency but risks losing task-relevant information. Through 18 controlled experiments, they show that MAS benefits weaker models or tasks where relay messages are near-sufficient, whereas stronger models often perform better in a single-agent setup that preserves the full, uncompressed context.

## [The Honest Quorum Problem: Epistemic Byzantine Fault Tolerance for Agentic Infrastructure](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16109v1)
**Score:** 0.884  |  **arXiv:** 2607.16109v1
**Length:** 26 pages

This paper addresses the "Honest Quorum Problem," where AI-based network validators reach consensus on semantically invalid state transitions due to shared reasoning errors (epistemic faults) despite strictly following protocol rules. To solve this, the authors introduce Epistemic Byzantine Fault Tolerance (EBFT), a new framework that augments traditional consensus models with mathematical bounds for reasoning safety and liveness degradation. The work provides quorum-threshold conditions and a calibration methodology, proving that simply adding more AI agents only improves security if it successfully reduces correlated reasoning failures.
