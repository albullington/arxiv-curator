## [When Do Multi-Agent Systems Help? An Information Bottleneck Perspective](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16133v1)
**Score:** 0.909  |  **arXiv:** 2607.16133v1

This paper introduces an information bottleneck framework to explain when and why multi-agent systems (MAS) outperform single-agent systems (SAS) in LLM tasks. The authors demonstrate that MAS limits context size through compressed relay messages, creating a fundamental trade-off where reducing context redundancy improves efficiency but risks losing task-relevant information. Through 18 controlled experiments, they show that MAS benefits weaker models or tasks where relay messages are near-sufficient, whereas stronger models often perform better in a single-agent setup that preserves the full, uncompressed context.

## [PagedWeight: Efficient MoE LLM Serving with Dynamic Quality-Aware Weight Quantization](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16184v1)
**Score:** 0.891  |  **arXiv:** 2607.16184v1

This paper introduces **PagedWeight**, a system designed to resolve the memory tension between Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) model weights and growing KV caches during LLM serving. By dynamically quantizing expert weights at runtime, it balances model precision with memory availability to optimize the tradeoffs between task accuracy, memory consumption, and throughput. Evaluation shows that PagedWeight achieves FP16-level accuracy with up to 72% GPU memory savings and a 1.94× throughput improvement over existing baselines.

## [The Honest Quorum Problem: Epistemic Byzantine Fault Tolerance for Agentic Infrastructure](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16109v1)
**Score:** 0.884  |  **arXiv:** 2607.16109v1
**Length:** 26 pages

This paper addresses the "Honest Quorum Problem," where AI-based network validators reach consensus on semantically invalid state transitions due to shared reasoning errors (epistemic faults) despite strictly following protocol rules. To solve this, the authors introduce Epistemic Byzantine Fault Tolerance (EBFT), a new framework that augments traditional consensus models with mathematical bounds for reasoning safety and liveness degradation. The work provides quorum-threshold conditions and a calibration methodology, proving that simply adding more AI agents only improves security if it successfully reduces correlated reasoning failures.

## [ToolSciVer: Multimodal Scientific Claim Verification with Visual Tool Augmented Reinforcement Learning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16131v1)

It equips VLMs with three specialized visual tools—table row/column focus, chart parsing, and high-resolution zooming—and trains the system using Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) to ensure accurate and efficient tool selection. Across multiple benchmarks, ToolSciVer significantly outperforms existing prompting- and reinforcement-learning-based baselines, making it highly relevant for researchers working on multimodal scientific reasoning.

## [CRAFT: Clustering Rubrics to Diagnose Weak LLM Capabilities and Generate Targeted Fine-Tuning Data](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16122v1)
**Score:** 0.865  |  **arXiv:** 2607.16122v1
**Length:** 18 pages

This paper introduces CRAFT, a method that analyzes rubric-based evaluation data to diagnose *why* a language model fails rather than just identifying *where* it fails. By clustering grading criteria into a hierarchical tree of capabilities, CRAFT pinpoints a model's specific weaknesses and uses these insights to generate targeted fine-tuning data. In experiments across finance and legal domains, models fine-tuned on CRAFT-generated data consistently outperformed those trained on baseline methods.

## [Understanding Reasoning from Pretraining to Post-Training](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16097v1)
**Score:** 0.853  |  **arXiv:** 2607.16097v1

Using chess and mathematics as controlled testbeds, this paper systematically investigates how pretraining configurations (such as model size and data volume) influence the effectiveness of subsequent reinforcement learning (RL). The authors demonstrate that a model's post-RL performance and rate of improvement are highly predictable from its pretraining loss and token count. Additionally, they show that RL does not merely sharpen existing behaviors, but actually surfaces novel, correct reasoning paths for difficult problems that the model rarely generated after supervised fine-tuning.

## [An Exam for Active Observers](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16165v1)
**Score:** 0.845  |  **arXiv:** 2607.16165v1
**Length:** 17 pages

This paper introduces **ActiveVision**, a new benchmark designed to evaluate whether multimodal large language models (MLLMs) can perform "active observation"—the human-like ability to iteratively and dynamically guide visual perception during a task. Testing reveals a massive performance gap: while human participants score an average of 96.1%, frontier models like GPT-5.5 and Claude Fable 5 fail on most tasks, scoring at most 10.6% even when allowed to write and run their own vision code. These results demonstrate that current MLLMs lack robust active perception, highlighting a critical need for architectures that tightly integrate visual perception and reasoning.

## [Evaluating Open-Weight LLMs for Generating Structured Threat Information for Autonomous Vehicle Vulnerabilities](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16175v1)

**Length:** 9 pages

To help secure connected and autonomous vehicles, this paper introduces **CAV-STIXGen**, a new dataset for mapping plain-text vulnerability descriptions (CVEs) to structured threat intelligence formats like STIX, CWE, and MITRE ATT&CK. The authors evaluate 11 open-weight LLMs (ranging from 4B to 120B parameters) to see how well they can automate this translation using different prompting strategies and multi-agent setups. The results show that while the models excel at identifying specific software weaknesses and threat objects (achieving F1 scores up to 0.99), mapping complete attack behaviors remains a significant challenge.
