## [Bridge Evidence: Static Retrieval Utility Does Not Predict Causal Utility in Multi-Step Agentic Search](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15253v1)
**Score:** 0.959  |  **arXiv:** 2607.15253v1

This paper reveals that traditional, static measures of document relevance are virtually uncorrelated with a document's actual causal usefulness in multi-step, agentic search. Through counterfactual testing on a language model agent, the authors identify "bridge documents"—texts that appear useless to a static evaluator but are actually crucial because they contain key entities that guide the agent’s subsequent queries. These findings demonstrate that optimizing retrieval systems for static relevance fails to improve multi-step agentic performance, highlighting a critical gap in how we evaluate search tools for AI agents.

## [Partition, Prompt, Aggregate: Statistical Self-Consistency in Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15277v1)
**Score:** 0.849  |  **arXiv:** 2607.15277v1

This paper investigates whether LLMs adhere to the law of total probability by testing if their aggregated estimates for subpopulations match their direct, population-level predictions. The authors find widespread violations of this self-consistency, notably discovering a "macro fallacy" where aggregating predictions from fine-grained subpopulations yields much more accurate results than querying the model about the general population directly. These findings reveal that LLMs struggle to propagate their internal subpopulation knowledge to aggregate estimates, establishing statistical self-consistency as a valuable, reference-free evaluation metric.

## [When Words Are Safe But Actions Kill: Probing Physical Danger Beyond Text Safety in Hidden-State Risk Space](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15218v1)
**Score:** 0.846  |  **arXiv:** 2607.15218v1

Leveraging this finding, the authors develop PRISM, a simple hidden-state probe that accurately detects physical risks while avoiding the over-blocking common in standard LLM safety judges. They also introduce PSB-1K, a new contrastive benchmark designed to evaluate physical risk detection without relying on explicit safety keywords.
