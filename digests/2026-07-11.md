## [Remember When It Matters: Proactive Memory Agent for Long-Horizon Agents](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08716v1)
**Score:** 0.946  |  **arXiv:** 2607.08716v1

To address the issue of agents losing track of key information during long-horizon tasks, this paper introduces a plug-and-play "proactive memory agent" that runs alongside any standard action agent. Instead of relying on passive retrieval, this auxiliary agent maintains a structured memory and actively decides when to inject timely reminders into the action agent's prompt or remain silent. Evaluated on long-context benchmarks like Terminal-Bench 2.0 and $\tau^2$-Bench, this selective intervention method significantly improves task success rates over passive or always-on memory baselines.

By decoupling base model capabilities from agent framework designs, this benchmark helps researchers precisely diagnose agent failure modes and understand how model and framework choices jointly impact real-world performance.

## [MPFlow: Learning Budgeted Max-Flow Optimization on the Lightning Network with Deep Graph Reinforcement Learning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08703v1)
**Score:** 0.866  |  **arXiv:** 2607.08703v1

This paper introduces **MPFlow**, a deep graph reinforcement learning framework designed to optimize liquidity placement on the Bitcoin Lightning Network by selecting which channels to open within a fixed budget. To maximize overall routing capacity, the model uses a message-passing policy trained with a novel "hub-exclusion" curriculum, forcing the agent to learn strategic, capacity-aware channel placements rather than simply connecting to the network's largest hubs. The approach consistently outperforms heuristic baselines on real network data and has been deployed in production, managing over $16 million in channel-allocation decisions across 30 nodes.

## [The Illusion of Equivalency: Statistical Characterization of Quantization Effects in LLMs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08734v1)
**Score:** 0.865  |  **arXiv:** 2607.08734v1

This paper demonstrates that standard metrics like accuracy and perplexity mask significant behavioral changes in quantized LLMs, creating an "illusion of equivalence" with their base models. To expose these hidden shifts, the authors introduce "correctness agreement," a new metric that measures the actual overlap in correct predictions between base and quantized models. Additionally, they analyze layer-wise distortions in attention weights, revealing that query and key projections are highly sensitive to quantization and identifying critical behavioral breakpoints at low bit-widths.

## [Super Weights in LLMs and the Failure of Selective Training](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08733v1)
**Score:** 0.831  |  **arXiv:** 2607.08733v1

This paper demonstrates that training "Super Weights"—highly critical individual parameters in LLMs—in isolation surprisingly collapses model performance to random levels, while training random weights in the same layers actually improves it. In contrast, structured methods like LoRA succeed even when explicitly restricted from updating these Super Weight coordinates. The findings show that a parameter's importance to model performance does not guarantee its trainability, indicating that effective fine-tuning requires structured, layer-wide updates rather than targeting individually key weights.

## [SLORR: Simple and Efficient In-Training Low-Rank Regularization](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08754v1)
**Score:** 0.831  |  **arXiv:** 2607.08754v1

To make neural networks more amenable to low-rank compression, this paper introduces SLORR, a simple and architecture-preserving training-time regularization framework. By using GPU-friendly approximations of Hoyer sparsity and the nuclear norm, SLORR directly regularizes weight matrices while avoiding expensive SVD computations or stateful caching. Empirical evaluations on ImageNet-1K and LLM pretraining demonstrate that SLORR-trained models retain significantly higher accuracy after compression while adding negligible training overhead (under 1% for LLMs).
